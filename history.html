Situated along the Malabar Coast, Tulunadu flourished as a cultural and trade hub, connecting India with Arabia, Africa, and Southeast Asia.

The Alupa Dynasty

One of the most significant ruling families of Tulunadu was the Alupa Dynasty, which governed the region for centuries (4th century CE to 15th century CE). Their rule saw the development of unique temple architecture, promotion of Tulu culture, and strong maritime trade networks. The capital shifted between Mangalore and Barkur over different periods.

Maritime Trade & Prosperity

Tulunaduâ€™s coastal position made it a thriving port region. Goods such as spices, areca nuts, coconuts, and rice were traded across oceans. Arab merchants left a lasting influence on the local culture, cuisine, and even vocabulary.

Cultural Heritage

The people of Tulunadu developed a distinct cultural identity with unique folk traditions such as Bhoota Kola, Nagamandala, and Kambala. Temples dedicated to various deities were built using indigenous architectural styles. Oral traditions kept the history and legends alive for generations.

Colonial Era

In the 16th century, Portuguese explorers and traders arrived, followed by the Mysore rulers under Hyder Ali and Tipu Sultan, and eventually the British. While colonial rule introduced modern education and infrastructure, it also altered traditional governance and trade patterns.

Modern Tulunadu

Today, Tulunadu spans parts of Dakshina Kannada, Udupi, and northern Kasaragod. Despite modern influences, it has preserved its language, rituals, and festivals. The people take pride in their heritage, ensuring Tulunaduâ€™s history is celebrated and passed on to future generations.
